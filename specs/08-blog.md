### Post 2: A Day in the Wakhan — Meeting the Kyrgyz of the Pamirs

**Category:** Travel Stories
**Estimated length:** 1,500 words
**Cover image:** Interior of a Kyrgyz yurt, morning light through the smoke hole

**Outline:**

**Opening:** Waking up in a yurt at 4,200 meters. Cold. The sound of yaks outside.

**Who the Kyrgyz are:** Brief history — descendants of groups who moved to the Pamir after the Soviet closure of the border. Stateless in a formal sense. A world of their own.

**A day in the camp:** Morning — tea and bread, milking yaks, loading horses. Midday — riding out to check the herd. Afternoon — felt-making with the women (if permitted). Evening — communal meal, a host who speaks no language the visitors share, and communication that works anyway.

**The question of change:** What do the Kyrgyz want? Are visitors welcome? Ali Reza's view on the ethics of visiting isolated communities.

**Photography note:** How to photograph people with dignity. The community consent practice Ali Reza uses.

**Closing:** Left the camp the next morning. Looked back once. Nothing visible in any direction except plateau and sky.

### Post 4: Afghan Cuisine — What to Eat and Where

**Category:** Culture & History
**Estimated length:** 900 words
**Cover image:** A spread of Afghan dishes on a cloth on the floor — qabuli, naan, yogurt, kebab

**Outline:**

**Opening:** Food is the fastest way into Afghan hospitality. The meal is never just a meal.

**The essential dishes:**
- Qabuli pulao — the national dish. Rice with lamb, carrots, raisins. How it's made, how it varies by region.
- Mantu — steamed dumplings with meat and yogurt. The preparation as a communal act.
- Bolani — flatbread stuffed with leek or potato. Street food and home food.
- Chapli kebab — Pashtun minced meat patty. Kandahari vs. Kabuli versions.
- Aushak — leek-stuffed pasta, boiled, with yogurt and meat sauce. Often confused with mantu.

**Regional variation:** What's different in the north (Uzbek-influenced), the Hazarajat, Kabul vs. the provinces.

**Tea culture:** Green tea vs. black tea. The role of tea in hospitality. How to accept it.

**Where to eat in Kabul:** A few specific recommendations by Ali Reza — not "restaurants" in a formal sense but places locals eat.

**Eating in a home:** What to expect. How to behave. Why it's the best meal you'll have.

**Related tours:** Kabul Deep Dive, Ancient Silk Road — Balkh & Mazar

### Post 5: Bamiyan Then and Now — Beyond the Buddhas

**Category:** Culture & History
**Estimated length:** 1,200 words
**Cover image:** The Buddha niches at dusk — the empty alcoves lit by last light

**Outline:**

**Opening:** The statues are gone. The valley remains.

**The history:** Bamiyan as a Buddhist civilization — 2,000 years of it. Monasteries carved into the cliffs. A Silk Road city. The scale of what was here.

**The destruction:** 2001. Not a long section — this is not the story. But it must be acknowledged.

**What remains:** The niches. The cave passages carved by monks. The Bamiyan Museum collection. The sheer scale of the cliff face.

**The living valley:** The Hazara community of Bamiyan today. The city is quiet, functional, and genuinely welcoming. A university. Markets. A generation growing up in the shadow of the niches.

**Dragon Valley:** The hidden canyon that most Bamiyan visitors don't reach. What it looks like inside.

**Why it's worth going:** The destruction of the Buddhas made Bamiyan famous. It is famous for the wrong reason. The right reason is the valley itself.

**Related tours:** Bamiyan Valley Cultural Trek, Band-e-Amir Lakes Discovery
